Electrostatics

$\vec{F} = \frac{1}{4 \pi \epsilon_0} \frac{Qq}{\lVert \vec{r} - \vec{r'} \rVert^2} \widehat{(\vec{r} - \vec{r'})}$     - Force between two electric charges
$\vec{E} = \frac{\vec{F}}{q} = -\nabla V$     - Definition of electric field and voltage

Note: $\nabla \cdot (\vec{E}V) = (\nabla \cdot \vec{E})V - \lVert \vec{E} \rVert^2$
$\Delta U = -W = q \Delta V = \frac{1}{2} \int_\mathcal{V} V dq$
$\mathfrak{u} = \frac{1}{2}\epsilon_0 E^2 + \int_0^P \vec{E} \cdot \vec{dP} = \frac{1}{2} \epsilon_0\epsilon_r E^2$     - Electric field energy density
$P = \frac{\sigma^2}{2\epsilon_0}$     - Pressure on the surface of a conductor due to charge distribution

Note: $\sigma = \epsilon_0 E$ for conductors
Dipoles:
  • $\vec{p} = q\vec{d}$
  • $V(\vec{r}) \approx \frac{\vec{p} \cdot \hat{r}}{4\pi\epsilon_0r^2}$
  • $\vec{\tau} = \vec{p} \times \vec{E}$
  • $U = -\vec{p} \cdot \vec{E}$
  • $\sum \vec{F} = \vec{p} \cdot (\nabla \vec{E})$
  • $\vec{E}(\vec{r}) = \frac{3(\vec{p} \cdot \hat{r})\hat{r} - \vec{p}}{4\pi\epsilon_0r^3}$
  • $U_{p_1, p_2} = \frac{3(\vec{p_1} \cdot \widehat{r_{21}})(\vec{p_2} \cdot \vec{r_{21})} + \vec{p_1} \cdot \vec{p_2}}{4\pi\epsilon_0r_{21}^3}$
  • For a conductor, $\vec{E} = 0 \implies \vec{p} = 0$ and $\vec{E} \neq 0 \implies \vec{p} \neq 0$ (and $\lVert \vec{p} \rVert \propto r^3E$)
Polarization
  • $\vec{P} = \frac{1}{\Delta V} \sum\limits_i \vec{p_i} = \epsilon_0\chi_e\vec{E}$     - Polarization = volume-averaged dipole moment
  • $\sigma_{b} = \vec{P} \cdot \hat{n} = P_{in}^\bot - P_{out}^\bot$ with $\sigma = \sigma_0 + \sigma_b$
  • $\rho_b = -\nabla \cdot \vec{P}$ with $\rho = \rho_0 + \rho_b$
  • $\rho_0 = \nabla \cdot \vec{D}$ where $\vec{D} = \epsilon_0 \vec{E} - \vec{P} = \epsilon_0 \epsilon_r \vec{E} = \frac{e_r}{e_r - 1}\vec{P}$ is the displacement vector
  • $\oint \vec{D} \cdot \vec{dS} = Q_0$
  • $\oint \vec{E} \cdot \vec{dS} = \frac{Q}{\epsilon_0}$     - Gauss' law
  • $\rho_0 = \epsilon_r \rho, \rho_b = (1 - \epsilon_r) \rho$
  • $\int \rho_b d^3r + \int \sigma_b dS = 0$     - Conservation of Charge
Clarification: Any terms above involving $e_r$ or $\chi_e$ assume we're acting in a linear media

Note: Because $\vec{E} = 0$ inside conductors and vacuums, $\vec{P} = 0$ as well (since $\vec{p} = 0$ when $\vec{E} = 0$)

Note on notation: Terms like $\sigma_0$ and $\rho_0$ above correspond to the free charge, and terms like $\sigma_b$ and $\rho_b$ correspond to the bias (induced) charge. The term $\sigma$ or $\rho$ referred to in other equations, such as Gauss' law, on this cheat sheet refer to the total charge ($\sigma = \sigma_0 + \sigma_b$ and $\rho = \rho_0 + \rho_b$).

Boundary Conditions
  • $E_2^\bot - E_1^\bot = \frac{\sigma}{\epsilon_0}$
  • $\vec{E}_1^\parallel = \vec{E}_2^\parallel$
  • $\Delta V = 0$
  • $D_2^\bot - D_1^\bot = \sigma_0$
  • $\vec{D}_2^\parallel - \vec{D}_1^\parallel = -(\vec{P}_2^\parallel - \vec{P}_1^\parallel)$

Magnetostatics

$\vec{F} = q(\vec{E} + \vec{v} \times \vec{B}) = I (\vec{l} \times \vec{B}) = \int \vec{J} \times \vec{B} d^3r$
$\vec{J} = \rho \vec{v}$     - Volume current density, $v$ = velocity of charge carrier

$\vec{K} = \sigma \vec{v}$    - Surface current density, $\sigma$ = conductivity = $\frac{1}{\rho}$

$\mathfrak{u} = \frac{B^2}{2\mu_0} = \frac{\vec{A} \cdot \vec{J}}{2}$     - Magnetic field energy density
$\vec{B} = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi} \int \frac{I \vec{dl} \times \vec{r}}{r^3} = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi} \int \frac{\vec{J}(\vec{r'}) \times \widehat{(\vec{r} - \vec{r'})}}{\lVert \vec{r} - \vec{r'} \rVert^2} d^3r'$     - Biot-Savart Law

Examples:
  • $\vec{B} = \frac{\mu_0I}{2\pi r} \hat{r}$ for infinite wires
  • $B = \mu_0 n I$ inside solenoids (direction depends on direction of current)
  • $B = \frac{\mu_0 NI}{2\pi r}$ inside toroids (direction depends on direction of current)
$\vec{B} = \nabla \times \vec{A}$ with $\nabla \cdot \vec{A} = 0 \implies \nabla^2 \vec{A} = -\mu_0 \vec{J}$     - Definition of magnetic potential $\vec{A}(\vec{r'}) = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi} \int \frac{\vec{J}(\vec{r'})}{\lVert \vec{r} - \vec{r'} \rVert} d^3r' = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi} \int \frac{I \vec{dl}}{\lVert \vec{r} - \vec{r'} \rVert} = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi} \int \frac{\vec{K}(\vec{r'})}{\lVert \vec{r} - \vec{r'} \rVert} dA'$

Note: $\oint \vec{A} \cdot \vec{dl} = \Phi_B$
Magnetic Dipoles
  • Multipole Expansion: $\vec{A}(\vec{r}) = \frac{\mu_0 I}{4\pi} \left[ \sum\limits_{n = 0}^\infty \frac{1}{r^{n+1}} \oint (r')^nP_n(\cos(\theta)) \vec{dr'} \right]$ where $P_n(\cos(\theta))$ is the $n^{\textrm{th}}$ Legendre polynomial
  • Dipoles:
    • $\vec{A}_{dipole}(\vec{r}) = \frac{\mu_0I}{4\pi} \frac{\vec{m} \times \hat{r}}{r^2}$ where $\vec{m} = \int I \vec{dS} = IS$ (for uniform currents)
    • $\vec{B}_{dipole}(\vec{r}) = \frac{\mu_0(3(\vec{m} \cdot \hat{r})\hat{r} - \vec{m})}{4\pi r^3}$
    • If $m$ is at the origin and in the $\hat{z}$ direction, $\vec{A}_{dipole}(\vec{r}) = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi} \frac{m\sin(\theta)}{r^2} \hat{\phi}$ and $\vec{B}_{dipole}(\vec{r}) = \frac{\mu_0m}{4\pi r^3}(2\cos(\theta) \hat{r} + \sin(\theta)) \hat{\theta}$
  • On one current loop: $\vec{\tau} = \vec{m} \times \vec{B}$ and $\sum \vec{F} = \nabla (\vec{m} \cdot \vec{B})$
Magnetization
  • $\vec{M} = \frac{1}{\Delta V} \sum\limits_i \vec{m_i}$     - Magnetization = volume-averaged magnetic dipole moment (on a bar magnet, $\vec{M}$ would point from the north pole to the south pole)
  • $\vec{K}_{b} = \vec{M} \times\hat{n}$ with $\vec{K}= \vec{K}_0 + \vec{K}_b$
  • $\vec{J}_b = \nabla \times \vec{M}$ with $\vec{J}= \vec{J}_0 + \vec{J}_b$
  • $\vec{J}_0 = \nabla \times\vec{H}$ where $\vec{H} = \frac{1}{\mu_0} \vec{B} - \vec{M} = \frac{1}{\chi_m} \vec{M} = \frac{1}{\mu_0(1+\chi_m)}\vec{B}$ is the displacement vector
    • Note: $\mu_0(1+\chi_m)$ is often just called $\mu$ as the magnetic permeability coefficient
  • $\oint \vec{H} \cdot \vec{dl} = I_0$
  • $\oint \vec{B} \cdot \vec{dl} = \mu_0I$     - Ampere's law
  • $\vec{J}_b = \chi_m \vec{J}_0$
  • $\nabla \cdot \vec{H} = - \nabla \cdot \vec{M} = \vec{B} \cdot (\nabla \frac{1}{\mu})$ at boundaries
  • $\int \vec{J}_b d^3r + \int \vec{K}_b dS = 0$     - Conservation of charge
Clarification: Any terms above involving $\chi_e$ assume we're acting in a linear media

Note: Terms like $\vec{K}_0$ and $\vec{J}_0$ above correspond to the free current, and terms like $\vec{K}_b$ and $\vec{J}_b$ correspond to the bias (induced) current. The term $\vec{K}$ or $\vec{J}$ referred to in other equations, such as Ampere's law, on this cheat sheet refer to the total current ($\vec{K} = \vec{K}_0 + \vec{K}_b$ and $\vec{J} = \vec{J}_0 + \vec{J}_b$).

Note:
  • In paramagnetic materials, $\vec{m} \parallel \vec{B}$ ($\vec{m}$ follows the rising $\nabla \vec{B}$ field) ($\chi_m > 0$)
  • In diamagnetic materials, $\vec{m} \upharpoonleft \! \downharpoonright \vec{B}$ ($\vec{m}$ follows the falling $\nabla \vec{B}$ field) ($\chi_m < 0$)
  • In ferromagnetic materials, $\vec{m}$ is preserved even after $\vec{B}$ is removed
Boundary Conditions
  • $B_1^\bot = B_2^\bot$
  • $H_2^\bot - H_1^\bot = -(M_2^\bot - M_1^\bot)$
  • $\vec{H}_2^\parallel - \vec{H}_1^\parallel = \vec{K}_0 \times \hat{n}$ (if $M = 0, \frac{1}{\mu_1}\vec{B}_2^\parallel - \frac{1}{\mu_2}\vec{B}_1^\parallel = \vec{K}_0 \times \hat{n}$)

Electrodynamics

$\vec{J} = \sigma \vec{f} = \sigma (\vec{E} + \vec{v} \times \vec{B}) \approx \sigma \vec{E}$     - Volume current density (defined so that $I = \int \vec{J} \cdot \vec{dS}$) ($f$ is the force per unit charge)

Note: $\vec{f} = \vec{f}_s + \vec{E} \implies \mathcal{E} = \oint \vec{f} \cdot \vec{dl} = \oint \vec{f}_s \cdot \vec{dl}$, where $\vec{f}_s$ is the force per unit charge from the source. For ideal batteries, $\vec{f} = 0 \implies \vec{E} = -\vec{f}_s$.

Note: In dynamic situations, the actual volume current density is $\vec{J} = \vec{J}_0 + \vec{J}_b + \vec{J}_p$, where $\vec{J}_p = \frac{\partial \vec{P}}{\partial t}$
Maxwell's Equations
  • $\oint \vec{E} \cdot \vec{dS} = \frac{Q_{encl}}{\epsilon_0} \implies \nabla \cdot \vec{E} = \frac{\rho}{\epsilon_0}$     (in a medium, it often helps to instead use: $\oint \vec{D} \cdot \vec{dS} = Q_{0, encl} \implies \nabla \cdot \vec{D} = \rho_0$)
  • $\oint \vec{B} \cdot \vec{dS} = 0 \implies \nabla \cdot \vec{B} = 0$
  • $\mathcal{E} = \oint \vec{E} \cdot \vec{dl} = -\frac{\partial \Phi_B}{\partial t} \implies \nabla \times \vec{E} = -\frac{\partial B}{\partial t}$
  • $\oint \vec{B} \cdot \vec{dl} = \mu_0 I_{encl} + \mu_0\epsilon_0\frac{\partial \Phi_E}{\partial t} \implies \nabla \times \vec{B} = \mu_0\vec{J} + \mu_0\epsilon_0 \frac{\partial \vec{E}}{\partial t}$     (in a medium, it often helps to instead use: $\oint \vec{H} \cdot \vec{dl} = I_{0, encl} + \frac{\partial \Phi_D}{\partial t}$, where $\Phi_D = \int \vec{D} \cdot \vec{dS}$)
Inductance (for a single loop of wire)
  • Mutual Inductance: $\Phi_2 = M_{21}I_1, \mathcal{E}_2 = -\frac{\partial \Phi_2}{\partial t} = -M \frac{dI_1}{dt}$
  • Self Inductance: $\Phi = LI, \mathcal{E} = -L \frac{dI}{dt}$
Note: For multiple loops of wire (ex. toroids, solenoids), multiply the induced EMF by the number of loops
Alfuen's Theorem:
For a moving loop (with velocity $\vec{v}$): $\frac{\partial \vec{B}}{\partial t} = \frac{1}{\mu_0\sigma}\nabla^2\vec{B} + \nabla \times (\vec{v} \times \vec{B})$
$\vec{S} = \frac{1}{\mu_0} (\vec{E} \times \vec{B})$     - Poynting vector (the power per unit time per unit area [aka intensity] of the EM field)

Note: if no work is done on the system, $\frac{\partial \mathfrak{u}}{\partial t} = -\nabla \cdot \vec{S}$
Maxwell Stress Tensor
  • $T_{xx} = \frac{1}{2}\epsilon_0(E_x^2-E_y^2-E_z^2) + \frac{1}{2\mu_0}(B_x^2-B_y^2-B_z^2)$
  • $T_{xy} = \epsilon_0E_xE_y + \frac{1}{\mu_0}B_xB_y$
  • $T_{ij}$ = force per area in the $i^{\textrm{th}}$ direction on an element of the surface oriented in the $j^\textrm{th}$ direction
$\vec{f} = \nabla \cdot \overset\leftrightarrow{T} - \epsilon_0\mu_0\frac{\partial \vec{S}}{\partial t} \implies \vec{F} = \oint_\mathcal{S} \overset\leftrightarrow{T} \cdot \vec{dA} - \epsilon_0\mu_0 \frac{\partial}{\partial t} \int_\mathcal{V} S d^3r$
Conservation of Momentum
  • $\vec{F} = \frac{d\vec{p}_{mech}}{dt} \implies \vec{p} = \int_\mathcal{V} \vec{g} d^3r = \textrm{momentum of the EM field}$ where $\vec{g} = \epsilon_0\mu_0\vec{S} = \epsilon_0 (\vec{E} \times \vec{B})$ is the momentum density for EM fields
  • If $\vec{p}_{mech}$ is constant (ex. in empty space), $\frac{\partial \vec{g}}{\partial t} = \nabla \cdot \overset\leftrightarrow{T}$
  • $\vec{l} = \vec{r} \times \vec{g} = \epsilon_0 (\vec{r} \times (\vec{E} \times \vec{B}))$      - Angular momentum density of EM fields

Electromagnetic Waves & Radiation

Wave Equation

$\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial z^2} = \frac{1}{v^2}\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial t^2} \implies \widetilde{f}(z, t) = \widetilde{A}e^{i(kz-\omega t)} \hat{n}$ for transverse waves ($\hat{n} = \hat{x}$ for horizontal polarization or $\hat{n} = \hat{y}$ for vertical polarization)

For light:
  • $\nabla^2 f = \frac{1}{v^2}\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial t^2}$ where $v = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\mu\epsilon}} = \frac{c}{n}$, where $n = \sqrt{\frac{\mu\epsilon}{\mu_0\epsilon_0}} \approx \sqrt{\epsilon_r}$ is the index of refraction (this is because $\mu \approx \mu_0$ for most materials)
  • $\vec{\widetilde{E}}(\vec{r}, t) = \widetilde{E}_0e^{i(\vec{k} \cdot \vec{r} - \omega t)} \hat{n}$ and $\vec{E}(\vec{r}, t) = \Re(\vec{\widetilde{E}}(\vec{r}, t))$
  • $\vec{\widetilde{B}}(\vec{r}, t) = \frac{1}{v}(\hat{k} \times \vec{\widetilde{E}})$ and $\vec{B}(\vec{r}, t) = \Re(\vec{\widetilde{B}}(\vec{r}, t))$
Wave Energy
  • Wave Intensity: $\vec{S} = \frac{1}{\mu_0}(\vec{E} \times \vec{B}) \implies \langle\vec{S}\rangle = \frac{1}{2}c\epsilon_0E_0^2 \hat{z}$
  • Wave Energy Density: $\mathfrak{u} = \epsilon_0E_0^2 \implies \langle\mathfrak{u}\rangle = \frac{1}{2}\epsilon_0E_0^2$
  • Wave Linear Momentum: $\vec{g} = \frac{1}{c^2}\vec{S} = \frac{\mathfrak{u}}{c} \hat{z} \implies \langle\vec{g}\rangle = \frac{1}{2c}\epsilon_0E_0^2 \hat{z}$
Radiation Pressure
  • $P = \frac{\langle S\rangle}{c}$ for a perfectly absorbing surface
  • $P = \frac{2\langle S\rangle}{c}$ for a perfectly reflecting surface
Wave Behavior at Boundaries


Boundary Conditions:
  • $\epsilon_1E_1^\bot = \epsilon_2E_2^\bot$
  • $\vec{E}_1^\parallel = \vec{E}_2^\parallel$
  • $B_1^\bot = B_2^\bot$
  • $\frac{1}{\mu_1}\vec{B}_1^\parallel = \frac{1}{\mu_2}\vec{B}_2^\parallel \implies \vec{B}_1^\parallel \approx \vec{B}_2^\parallel$
For all waves:
  • $k_1 = k_R = \frac{n_1}{n_2} k_T$
  • $\theta_1 = \theta_R$     - Law of Reflection
  • $n_1 \sin(\theta_1) = n_2(\theta_2)$     - Law of Refraction (aka Snell's Law)
  • The reflected wave undergoes a 180° phase shift if $n_2 > n_1$
p-polarization ($\vec{B} \parallel \textrm{boundary}$) s-polarization ($\vec{E} \parallel \textrm{boundary}$)
$R = \left(\frac{E_R}{E_1}\right)^2 = \left(\frac{n_2\cos(\theta_1) - n_1\cos(\theta_2)}{n_2\cos(\theta_1) + n_1\cos(\theta_2)}\right)^2$ $R = \left(\frac{E_R}{E_1}\right)^2 = \left(\frac{n_1\cos(\theta_1) - n_2\cos(\theta_2)}{n_1\cos(\theta_1) + n_2\cos(\theta_2)}\right)^2$
$T = \frac{n_2}{n_1}\left(\frac{E_T}{E_1}\right)^2\frac{\cos(\theta_2)}{\cos(\theta_1)} = \frac{4n_1n_2\cos(\theta_1)\cos(\theta_2)}{(n_2\cos(\theta_1) + n_1\cos(\theta_2))^2}$ $T = \frac{n_2}{n_1}\left(\frac{E_T}{E_1}\right)^2\frac{\cos(\theta_2)}{\cos(\theta_1)} = \frac{4n_1n_2\cos(\theta_1)\cos(\theta_2)}{(n_1\cos(\theta_1) + n_2\cos(\theta_2))^2}$
